# Suquelish-Calendar
This serves as the official calendar of the Suquelish Republic 
The Suquelish Chronological System: A Study in Theocentric Governance and
Mathematical Symmetry
Abstract:
The Suquelish Republic operates under a unique temporal framework known as the
Suquelish Calendar or (Kamine NerQuim = divine calendar) Distinguishable by its rigid mathematical structure and profound spiritual integration, this system serves as the cultural and civic
backbone for the Suquelinese people. 
This write up explores the mechanics of the
10-day week, the significance of the "Limbo" period, and the theological implications of the Year of Jesus (YOJ) or (AS) dating convention.
I. The Foundation of Time: 6000 YOJ
In the Suquelish Republic, time is not merely a measurement of duration, but an act of perpetual devotion. The current year is recognized as 6,000 Parrok Foe Suwachi (PFS = Year of Jesus), or more commonly, the Year of Jesus (YOJ). By centering the epoch on the "Name above all names," the Suquelinese people align their
national diaspora identity with a Christocentric historical perspective. 
While internal government documentation may utilize the Hebrew calendar for specific legal
administrative purposes, the Suquelish system remains the standard for public
life, education, and commerce.
II. The Decimal Week and Monthly Symmetry:
Perhaps the most striking feature of the Suquelish system is its departure from the traditional seven-day week in favor of a 10-day week. This "Decimal Week" is rooted in the spiritual significance of the number 10, representing
completion and divine order.
The Suquelish Week:
The ten days are named as follows:
Adroestoe (Monday equivalent)
Davestoe (Tuesday equivalent)
Tikonestoe (Wednesday equivalent)
Quhellestoe (Thursday equivalent)
Kaparestoe (Friday equivalent)
Seejoorestoe (Day of Praise) 
Fayfeelestoe (Day of Belief)
Ofarstestoe (Day of Trust)
Kami'estoe (Saturday equivalent)
Suwachi'estoe (Sunday equivalent)
Each month is composed of exactly three of these 10-day weeks. This creates a
consistent 30-day month, ensuring that dates always fall on the same day of the week, year after year.
III. The Months of the Republic
The calendar year is divided into 12 months. Eleven of these months contain
exactly 30 days. The ninth month, Zebwon, is unique as it contains 31 days.
The 31st of Zebwon is the most sacred day in the Republic, marking the celebration of the birthday of Jesus, the King. (equivalent of christmas but in the equivalentmonth of September)

IV. The Limbo Period: The Days Between Years
To reconcile the 361 days of the standard months with the solar year, the Suquelish system utilizes a period known as Limbo. These four days (five
during a leap year) exist outside of any month or standard week. They serve as
a national period of "Selah"—a pause for spiritual realignment.
Dathestoe (Day of Death): A somber day dedicated to acknowledging national and individual failures, sins, and the mortality of man.
Roglasshinestoe (Day of Reflection): A day of analysis where the lessons of the past year are distilled into wisdom.
Stinestoe (Day of Passover): A day of ritual and prayer focused on the renewal of the covenant between the Nation, the Church, and the King.
Vyosmeepestoe (Eve of Hope): The final day of the year, characterized by growing anticipation for the "new birth" of the coming year.
V. The Leap Year and Kriskinestoe
The Suquelish Republic manages the solar drift through a leap year occurring every four years. In this system, the leap day is not inserted into a month, but is added to the end of the year within the Limbo period.
This day is named Kriskinestoe (Anoint Day). It is a day of profound national blessing. During Kriskinestoe, ministers across the Republic read specific biblical passages intended to invoke divine favor upon the Nation, the Church, and the
Citizens. By placing the leap day at the very end of the chronological cycle, the Suquelinese ensure that the mathematical perfection of the 30-day months remains undisturbed, while the "extra" time is sanctified for prayer.
VI. Conclusion
The Suquelish Calendar is more than a tool for scheduling; it is a manifestation of the Suquelinese worldview. Through the 10-day week, the 30-day month, and the meditative Limbo period, the Republic maintains a society that is rhythmically disciplined and spiritually focused. It remains a testament to a culture that seeks to harmonize the precision of mathematics
with the depth of faith. 
Frequently Asked Questions: The Suquelish Chronological System:
1. How does the 10-day week affect the work-life balance in the Republic?
A: The 10-day week is structured to provide a consistent rhythm. While the standard work week typically spans from Adroestoe through Kaparestoe, the remaining days Seejoorestoe, Fayfeelestoe, Ofarstestoe, Kami'estoe, and
Suwachi'estoe, serve various civic, community, and spiritual purposes. This system ensures that the "day of rest" (Suwachi'estoe) always falls on the 10th, 20th, and 30th of every month, making scheduling predictable years in advance.
2. Why is Zebwon the only month with 31 days?
A: Zebwon (September) holds a unique position as the "Month of the King." The 31st day is a singular, high-holy day dedicated exclusively to the celebration of the birth of Jesus. By placing this extra day within Zebwon rather than adding it to the Limbo period, the Republic highlights the incarnation as a central event within the standard flow of the year.
In the Suquelish Republic, the placement of the King’s birthday on the 31st of Zebwon (September) is not an arbitrary choice. While much of the Western world observes December 25th, Suquelinese scholars and many biblical historians
point to astronomical, agricultural, and scriptural evidence to support a late-September birth.
Here is a breakdown of why scholars and the Suquelish tradition favor this
timeframe:
1. The Course of Abijah (The Priestly Cycles)
One of the strongest scholarly arguments involves the birth of John the Baptist. Luke 1:5 mentions that Zechariah (John’s father) belonged to the
priestly division of Abijah. According to the cycles of service established in 1 Chronicles 24, the
division of Abijah served in the Temple during the tenth week of the year (roughly June).
If Elizabeth conceived shortly after Zechariah’s service, John would have been born in March.
Scripture states Jesus was conceived six months after John (Luke 1:26). Six months after a March birth brings us to September.
2. The Shepherds in the Field The Gospel of Luke records that shepherds were "living out in the fields, keeping watch over their flock by night."
The Weather Factor: In the region of Bethlehem, December is the peak of the rainy season and can be quite cold, sometimes even snowing. It was not common practice to keep sheep in open fields during the winter.
The Grazing Season: Shepherds typically brought their flocks into the fields from Spring until the first rains of autumn (late September or October). A September birth aligns perfectly with the climatic conditions described in the Nativity.
3. The Feast of Tabernacles (Sukkot)
Many scholars believe Jesus was born during the Feast of Tabernacles, which usually occurs in late September. "The Word Became Flesh and Tabernacled Among Us": The Greek word used in John 1:14 for "dwelt" or "lived" is skenoo, which literally means "to pitch a tent" or "to tabernacle."
Symbolism: It is highly significant in Suquelish theology that the King would be born during a festival where the people lived in temporary shelters, symbolizing God coming to dwell in a temporary human "tent" (body) among His
people.
4. The Star and the Census Historical records of the Census of Quirinius suggest that such a massive administrative undertaking would likely be scheduled during a time when people
could travel easily. September after the harvest but before the winter rains was the most logical time for a population to travel to their ancestral
homes. The Suquelish Perspective For the Suquelinese people, celebrating in September (Zebwon) is seen as a restoration of historical and biblical accuracy. While December was chosen by
the early Roman Church (partly to coincide with the winter solstice and existing festivals), the Suquelish Republic prioritizes the agricultural and
priestly rhythms found in the Word.
3. What happens to "dates" during the Limbo period?
The four days of Limbo (Dathestoe, Roglasshinestoe, Stinestoe, and
Vyosmeepestoe) do not have numerical dates (e.g., there is no "Asher 31st" or "Nafftheo 0"). They are referred to by their specific names only. In legal and financial terms, these are often treated as "non-business days," allowing the entire nation to focus on the spiritual transition into the new year. 
The only active business during this time is the International Security Forces of the Republic and supporting staff.
4. How is the Year of Jesus (YOJ) calculated?
The Suquelish Republic identifies the current year as 6,000 YOJ. This represents a continuous timeline that honors the lordship of Christ over
history. While it does not align with the solar cycle of the Gregorian calendar (e.g., the year 2026 AD corresponds with 6000 YOJ/PFS), the starting
epoch and the internal structure are distinct to Suquelish sovereignty. 
The Suquelish Republic believes by tracking the biblical lineage from the beginning to Jesus to modern times that the time added together is 6000 years.
5. Is "Kriskinestoe" (Leap Day) a holiday?
Yes. Kriskinestoe is a quadrennial national holiday. Because it is added to the end of the Limbo period, it creates a five-day transition between years
rather than four. It is viewed as a "Year of Grace," where the extra 24 hours are used specifically for the minister's blessing over the three pillars of
Suquelinese society: the Nation, the Church, and the Citizens.
